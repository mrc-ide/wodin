Exploring Disease Spread with the NPI Simulator

Epidemiological models are essential tools for understanding how infectious diseases spread and how interventions influence their course. The NPI (Non-Pharmaceutical Interventions) Simulator is designed to allow users to interactively explore these dynamics by modifying key parameters and observing how disease transmission changes in response to different public health measures.

This page integrates interactive visualizations, adjustable model components, and scenario comparisons to provide an immersive experience in epidemic modeling. As you scroll, you will engage with simulations demonstrating disease progression, intervention effectiveness, and sensitivity analyses, offering deeper insight into the complexity of outbreak dynamics.

Modeling Disease Transmission

Infectious disease models are built upon a framework of assumptions about how individuals move through different stages of infection and how they interact with others in a population. These models aim to capture the transition of individuals between health states – such as being susceptible to infection, becoming infected (and therefore infectious to others), before either recovering or experiencing more severe outcomes, such as hospitalisation and/or death. The models represent how these individual transitions between health states contribute to the overall spread of the disease

The tool simulates how a respiratory virus spreads within an age-structured population. People start in the “susceptible” category (S), meaning they are healthy but at risk of catching the disease. If they are exposed to someone infectious, they move into the “exposed” group (E), where they carry the virus but are not yet symptomatic. From here, they either develop symptoms and move into the “symptomatic infected” group (I), or they stay without symptoms and move into the “asymptomatic” group (A). Asymptomatic individuals all eventually recover (R), but symptomatic people might be hospitalized (H), and hospitalized people can either recover (R) or die (D). The age of an individual impacts their risk of different disease outcomes as well as the impact of different NPIs. The model tracks and outputs how many people are in each disease stage over time.

Key Epidemiological Parameters and Their Impact

The course of an outbreak is dictated by fundamental epidemiological parameters, which influence transmission, disease severity, and recovery dynamics.

The basic reproduction number ($R_{0}$) quantifies the average number of secondary infections generated by a single infectious case in a fully susceptible population. A higher $R_{0}$ indicates faster epidemic growth, whereas an $R_{0}$ below one signals a decline in transmission.

Understanding how disease transmission evolves in a population requires more than just the reproduction number. While $R_{0}$ gives an estimate of how many individuals a single case will infect on average, the severity and burden of an outbreak are also influenced by hospitalization and infection fatality rates. These parameters determine how many cases require medical intervention and how many progress to severe disease, ultimately affecting the capacity of healthcare systems and mortality rates.

Hospitalization rates reflect the probability that an infected individual will develop severe disease requiring clinical care. This varies by pathogen and demographic characteristics, such as age and comorbidities. Similarly, infection fatality rates indicate the proportion of infections that result in death, helping policymakers estimate potential mortality burdens and plan healthcare responses accordingly.
